What Is Color Theory in Design?

Master the art and science of color interaction

Color theory is the study of how colors interact with each other and how they affect our perception. It covers color relationships, color harmony, and the psychological impact of color. Colors can be classified as either primary, secondary, or tertiary.

Color Classification

Primary Colors

Yellow, blue, and red are the fundamental hues from which all other colors are derived.

Red
Blue
Yellow

Secondary Colors

Secondary colors result from mixing equal amounts of two primary colors. Green, orange, and purple are examples of secondary colors.

Orange
Purple
Green

E.g., Yellow + Blue = Green

Tertiary Colors

Tertiary colors result from combining a primary color with a neighboring secondary color. Yellow-Green, Blue-Green, and Blue-Violet are examples.

Temperature & Psychology

Colors can be classified as warm or cool based on their "temperature".

Warm Colors

Reds, oranges, yellows. Evoke comfort, warmth, and coziness.

Cool Colors

Blues, greens, purples. Evoke calmness, serenity, and professionalism.

Color Models

Models are essential for describing and reproducing colors in a standard way. Frequently used models include:

  • RGB: Red, Green, Blue (Digital screens)
  • HSV: Hue, Saturation, Value
  • HSL: Hue, Saturation, Lightness

The Color Wheel

A circular diagram showing how colors relate. It helps designers select combinations and create color palettes.

Color Schemes

Analogous

Adjacent colors on the wheel. Cohesive and soothing.

Complementary

Opposite ends of the wheel. High contrast and visual impact.

Triadic

Approximately equidistant. Vibrant and balanced.

Monochromatic

Derived from the same base color. Unity and harmony.

Contrast & Accessibility

Color contrast ensures text and important elements are distinguishable from their background. This is crucial for web accessibility and users with visual disabilities.
